## SEQUENCE STRATEGY OVERVIEW

### The Narrative Arc

These 6 emails tell one continuous story. Each email is self-contained (a reader can get value from any single email), but the sequence builds a cumulative argument that makes PeakHer feel inevitable by email 6.

| Email | Day | Subject Line | Core Story Section | Narrative Role |
|-------|-----|-------------|-------------------|----------------|
| 1 | Day 0 | The stat that changed how I think about women's performance | Market data (Section 1) | **The Wake-Up Call** -- establish credibility, name the hidden problem |
| 2 | Day 2 | Why you crush it one week and crash the next (it's not what you think) | Reasons #1-2 | **The Explanation** -- give her language for what she's felt but never understood |
| 3 | Day 5 | You've optimized everything except the one thing that affects everything | Reasons #3-4 | **The Mirror** -- show her the gap she didn't know existed |
| 4 | Day 7 | The system was never built for your biology | Reason #5 | **The Reframe** -- shift blame from herself to the system |

| 6 | Day 12 | What it looks like when you finally have the data | Product + Offer (Section 8) | **The Vision** -- paint the picture, reveal the product, invite to waitlist |

### The Psychology

- **Emails 1-2:** Pattern interrupt. "I thought I understood my performance. Maybe I don't."
- **Emails 3-4:** Pain deepening with compassion. "This isn't my fault. The system wasn't built for me."
- **Email 5:** Self-diagnosis. "Now I know exactly where I stand."
- **Email 6:** Resolution. "There's a tool that solves this. I want in."

The sequence follows the Chet Holmes "Stadium Pitch" model: 80% education, 20% transition to offer. The education IS the selling. By the time the product appears in email 6, it feels like the obvious answer to a problem they now deeply understand.

## POST-SEQUENCE STRATEGY

### What Happens After Email 6

The 6-email sequence is the education phase. What follows depends on the reader's behavior:

**Path A: Joined the Waitlist (beta-pipeline)**

- Pre-launch: Early access invitation with founding member pricing. 48-hour priority window before public launch.
- Post-launch: Onboarding sequence (separate from marketing -- focused on getting value in the first 7 days).

**Path B: Engaged But Did Not Join Waitlist (warm-passive)**
- Day 15: "Quick question" email. Subject: "Did anything from the past two weeks resonate?" Body: conversational, one question, easy to reply. No hard CTA.
- Day 21: Case study or testimonial email (once beta users produce results). Subject: "What [Name] discovered in her first month with PeakHer."
- Day 30: New research or insight email. Fresh content, not a rehash of the sequence. Position as ongoing value.
- Day 45: "Doors opening" email if/when beta opens a new cohort. Soft scarcity.

**Path C: Low Engagement (cold-passive)**
- Move to monthly newsletter immediately after sequence ends.
- Re-engage in 30 days with a completely different angle (e.g., a specific tactical tip rather than the full framework).

**Path D: Replied to Any Email (hot-lead)**
- Manual follow-up within 24 hours. Personal, not templated.
- If reply was a question: answer thoroughly, offer to continue the conversation.
- If reply was positive ("this resonates"): thank her, share the waitlist link, offer a call if appropriate.
- If reply was skeptical: address the specific concern directly, share relevant research, respect her position.

## SUBJECT LINE BEST PRACTICES FOR THIS AUDIENCE

### Who She Is

The target reader is an ambitious, high-performing woman -- likely a professional, entrepreneur, athlete, or some combination. She is smart, skeptical, and overwhelmed with marketing emails. She deletes 90% of what hits her inbox.

### What Works

1. **Data-driven curiosity.** Lead with a specific number or finding. "81% of women report this" outperforms "The truth about women's performance." Specificity signals substance.

2. **Pattern interrupts.** Challenge something she believes about herself. "Why you crush it one week and crash the next" works because she's experienced it but never named it. She opens to find out if you have the answer.

3. **Validation without flattery.** "Most high-performing women score between 11 and 20 on this" works because it validates her identity (high-performing) while creating curiosity (what's the score?). Never patronize. Never "girlboss."

4. **Lowercase, conversational tone.** No ALL CAPS. No excessive punctuation. No "YOU WON'T BELIEVE THIS." Write subject lines the way you'd text a friend who happens to be brilliant.

5. **Avoid trigger words.** "Period," "menstrual," "hormones" in subject lines will lose 80% of the audience before they open. Use "performance," "energy," "rhythm," "biology," "patterns." The subject line gets them in the door. The body educates.

6. **Keep it under 50 characters when possible.** Mobile truncation is real. "The stat that changed how I think about women's performance" is long but works because the first 40 characters carry the hook: "The stat that changed how I think about..."

7. **Test emotional vs. analytical.** This audience responds to both. A/B test "What if inconsistency is a signal, not a flaw?" (emotional) against "The 10x hormone swing nobody told you about" (analytical). Different segments of your audience will respond to different registers.

### What Does Not Work

- Hype language ("revolutionary," "game-changing," "unlock your potential")
- Gendered cliches ("queen," "boss babe," "girl power," "slay")
- Fear-based ("you're falling behind," "what you're doing wrong")
- Vague promises ("the secret to better performance," "what every woman needs to know")
- Product-first ("introducing PeakHer," "our new app," "check out our platform")

## UNSUBSCRIBE AND OPT-DOWN STRATEGY

### Standard Unsubscribe

Every email includes a standard unsubscribe link in the footer. This is non-negotiable and legally required.

### Opt-Down Option

In addition to full unsubscribe, offer an "opt-down" option starting in email 3. Language:

> "Getting too many emails? I get it. [Click here to switch to monthly updates only] -- you'll still get the best research and insights, just less often."

This preserves the relationship while respecting her inbox. An opt-down subscriber is infinitely more valuable than an unsubscriber.

### Frequency Expectations

Set expectations in the email 1 footer:

> "You'll receive 6 emails over the next 12 days, then one email per month. You can adjust your preferences or unsubscribe anytime."

This reduces unsubscribes by eliminating the "how many more of these am I going to get?" anxiety.

### Re-Permission After Inactivity

If a subscriber has not opened an email in 90 days, send a single re-permission email:

> Subject: "Should I keep sending these?"
> Body: "I noticed you haven't opened one of my emails in a while. No hard feelings -- I'd rather send to people who want to hear from me. [Click here to stay on the list] or do nothing and I'll remove you in 7 days."

This keeps the list healthy and deliverability high.
