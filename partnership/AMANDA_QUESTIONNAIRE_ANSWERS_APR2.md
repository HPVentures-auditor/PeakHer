## FULL ANSWERS

### Q1. First 100 Women Using PeakHer

28-34 year old knowledge workers in marketing, consulting, tech, product, healthcare, or finance. Hybrid or remote, with enough calendar autonomy to act on what PeakHer tells them. Urban or suburban, college-educated, $75K-$150K household, already spending $100-$150/month on wellness subscriptions.

iPhone users. Most own or are considering Oura Ring or Apple Watch. Current app stack is fragmented: Flo or Clue for tracking, MyFitnessPal for nutrition, Headspace for mental wellness, Notion for tasks, maybe Peloton Digital. None talk to each other, none account for hormones.

She already knows hormones affect performance. She's read *In the FLO* or follows hormone health creators on TikTok. She's tired of Googling "luteal phase workout" every month. She wants one place that says: here's what to eat, how to move, what to schedule, and why.

Key quote: "She's not the woman who needs convincing that female biology matters. She's the woman who's frustrated that every tool she uses was designed as if it doesn't."

### Q2. Lead Archetype: The Business Woman

Without hesitation. Calendar optimization, meeting prep coaching, task prioritization by phase, readiness score — these only matter if you have a calendar full of decisions. No competitor offers this. Flo doesn't. Wild.AI doesn't. MyFLO doesn't.

- Highest willingness to pay ($15-$20/mo vs. $3-$5 for generic trackers)
- Most likely to tell friends (networked, group chats, women-in-leadership circles)
- Makes positioning clear from Day 1 — "hormone intelligence for high-performing women," not a wellness app

Key quote: "When she says, 'I used PeakHer to schedule my board presentation during peak estrogen — when my verbal fluency and confidence are biologically highest — and I crushed it,' that story travels. That's not a product feature. That's a dinner party story."

Other archetypes come months 4-6, drawn by a brand that already means something specific.

### Q3. "Performance Intelligence" — Needs Tuning

Concept is right. Language needs work. "Performance intelligence" sounds like enterprise software.

**"Hormone intelligence"** is the answer. It sounds like a capability, not a category. Reframes physiology as an asset. Feels scientific without feeling clinical. Differentiates from "cycle tracking."

How Amanda would describe PeakHer to a friend: "It's hormone intelligence — it reads where you are hormonally and tells you exactly what to eat, how to train, what to schedule, and why. It turns your biology into your edge."

Positioning statement: "PeakHer is a hormone intelligence engine for women — translating the science of female physiology into daily, actionable optimization across nutrition, movement, productivity, and performance."

Tagline options from Amanda:
- "Your hormones. Your edge."
- "Hormone intelligence, daily."
- "Built for female physiology. Finally."
- "What your hormones want you to know today."

Key rule: Don't say "cycle." Say hormones, female physiology, biology, or physiology-driven intelligence.

### Q4. Missing Archetypes

**The Struggler:** PCOS, PMDD, endometriosis. Not optimizing — surviving. Deeply motivated, will be most vocal advocate. Active in communities (r/PCOS 100K+ members). "She'll post the tearful TikTok about the app that finally made me feel like my biology isn't broken." Build for her, don't lead with her.

**The Perimenopause Pioneer:** Age 38-48. Sleep disruption, energy crashes, brain fog, irregular cycles. Doctor says "labs are normal." Highest disposable income. Every hormone app targets 25-35 — she's invisible. Phase 2 expansion, not Day 1.

Skip: student (too young, low WTP), retiree (post-menopausal), new mom as distinct archetype (specialized, regulatory considerations).

### Q5. Check-in Friction

- Can't find login button on website
- Current branding feels basic, not advanced — doesn't match brand guide
- Wants journal entry options based on what people want to track (like Whoop)
- Ask in onboarding: training for anything? Intermittent fasting? So we can optimize training + fasting + cycle together

### Q6. AI Briefing Quality

"Pretty good actually." Expects it'll improve with calendar, to-do list, and wearable sync. Wants to pick her tone — "whether slightly unhinged or hype mode."

### Q7. The ONE Morning Thing

Two-part morning moment:

1. **7 AM notification from Dot** — not a reminder, a piece of hormone intelligence with an open loop. "Your progesterone just peaked. There's one thing you should move on your calendar today." Just enough to feel seen, not enough to feel satisfied. She HAS to open it.

2. **In-app brief** — Dot greets with the full story. What hormones are doing, why she feels what she feels, one clear action. Not a dashboard. Not five recommendations. One insight, one action, in the voice of a best friend who studied endocrinology. Rest is there if she scrolls.

Key quote: "That's not an app I use. That's an app I'd miss if it were gone."
